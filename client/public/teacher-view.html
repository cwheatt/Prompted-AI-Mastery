Quick Start Guide for Teachers

ðŸŽ¯ Get Your Students Started This Week

No extensive prep required. This guide provides a simple, actionable plan to introduce the Prompted platform in just three class sessions.

ðŸ“… Your 3-Day Implementation Plan

ðŸ“… Day 1: Baseline Assessment (15 minutes)

Objective: Students establish their current AI literacy level through self-reflection.

Begin by explaining to your students that artificial intelligence tools are becoming essential in education and careers, but using them effectively requires specific skills. Today they will take a brief self-assessment to understand their current relationship with AI tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, or Copilot.

Direct students to the AI Literacy Self-Assessment (Section 1 in the platform). Students can access this through the full workbook or the public preview page. They will rate themselves on twelve statements covering three key areas: their understanding of AI capabilities, their habits when using AI tools, and their ability to think critically about AI outputs.

After students calculate their scores, facilitate a brief class discussion. Ask volunteers to share which mastery level they achievedâ€”Emerging, Developing, or Strategicâ€”without requiring them to reveal specific scores. This creates psychological safety while normalizing the learning journey.

ðŸ’¡ Teacher tip: Take the assessment yourself before class and share your own results. This models vulnerability and demonstrates that AI literacy is a learning process for everyone.

ðŸ“… Day 2: The THINK Framework (20 minutes)

Objective: Students learn a practical five-step framework for responsible AI interaction.

Introduce the THINK Framework as a strategic approach to using AI tools. Explain that effective AI users do not simply accept whatever the tool generatesâ€”they apply critical thinking at every stage of the interaction.

T - Target Your Goal: Before opening an AI tool, students must clearly define what they want to accomplish.

H - Handle with Care: Students should treat AI tools as powerful assistants that require careful instruction.

I - Investigate the Output: Critical evaluation is essential. Students must read AI responses carefully.

N - Navigate Verification: Students need strategies for fact-checking AI outputs.

K - Keep Ownership: Students must maintain their role as the primary thinker and creator.

After presenting the framework, work through a live example as a class. Choose a common student task and demonstrate how to apply each THINK step. Invite students to suggest improvements to your prompts and evaluation strategies.

ðŸ’¡ Teacher tip: Create a visual anchor chart with the THINK Framework and post it prominently in your classroom. Students will reference this throughout the year.

ðŸ“… Day 3: Hands-On Practice (25 minutes)

Objective: Students apply the THINK Framework to a real AI interaction and reflect on the process.

Assign students a low-stakes task that requires AI interaction. Choose something aligned with your current curriculumâ€”for example, asking an AI tool to explain a concept you are studying, generate practice questions, or provide feedback on a draft paragraph.

Students should complete the AI Interaction Log (Section 3 in the platform) as they work. This structured worksheet guides them through documenting their goal, the prompt they used, the AI's response, their evaluation of that response, and the actions they took to verify or improve the output.

After students complete their logs, facilitate a reflection discussion. Ask questions like: What surprised you about the AI's response? How did your prompt affect the quality of the output? What verification strategies did you use?

ðŸ’¡ Teacher tip: Consider having students work in pairs for this first practice session. Collaborative problem-solving helps students learn from each other's approaches.

Understanding AI Bias

ðŸ§  How AI Bias Develops

  1. Training Data Bias: AI learns from existing data that reflects historical biases
  2. Selection Bias: Training data doesn't represent all groups equally
  3. Measurement Bias: The way data is collected favors certain outcomes
  4. Aggregation Bias: One model is used for groups with different needs
  5. Evaluation Bias: Testing doesn't include diverse populations

ðŸš¨ Real-World Examples

  • Hiring Tools: Amazon's AI recruiting tool showed bias against women because it was trained on resumes from male-dominated tech industry
  • Facial Recognition: Systems have higher error rates for people of color, leading to false arrests
  • Healthcare: Algorithms underestimate health needs of Black patients because they use healthcare spending as proxy for health needs
  • Criminal Justice: Risk assessment tools show racial bias in predicting recidivism
  • Language Models: Generate stereotypical associations (e.g., "nurse" with "she", "engineer" with "he")

ðŸ” Student Activity: Bias Detection

Instructions:

  1. Ask AI to describe a "successful businessperson"
  2. Ask AI to describe a "nurse" or "teacher"
  3. Ask AI to generate an image of a "scientist" or "CEO"
  4. Analyze: What assumptions does AI make?
  5. Discuss: Where might these biases come from?
  6. Reflect: How can we counteract bias in our prompts?

Privacy & Data Concerns

âš ï¸ Critical Rule: Never share personal information, private details, or confidential data with AI tools.

ðŸ“Š What Happens to Your Data

  • Training Data: Your inputs may be used to train future AI models
  • Storage: Conversations are typically stored on company servers
  • Analysis: Companies analyze usage patterns and content
  • Sharing: Data may be shared with third parties (read terms of service)
  • Permanence: Even "deleted" data may remain in backups

ðŸš« Never Share With AI

  • Full names, addresses, phone numbers
  • Social Security numbers or ID numbers
  • Passwords or login credentials
  • Financial information (credit cards, bank accounts)
  • Medical information or health details
  • Private family information
  • Other people's personal information
  • Confidential school or work information

âœ… Safe AI Use Practices

  • Use school-approved tools: Your school has vetted these for safety
  • Anonymize examples: Use "Student A" instead of real names
  • Keep it general: Discuss concepts, not personal situations
  • Check privacy settings: Understand what data is collected
  • Ask permission: Before sharing others' work or ideas with AI

Academic Integrity & AI

Core Principle: Using AI is not cheatingâ€”but how you use it can be.

âœ… Ethical AI Use (Generally Acceptable)

  • Brainstorming: Generate ideas to consider
  • Research: Find information to verify elsewhere
  • Explanation: Understand difficult concepts
  • Feedback: Get suggestions for improvement
  • Editing: Check grammar and clarity (your own writing)
  • Examples: See how concepts apply
  • Practice: Generate practice problems

Key: You maintain ownership and do the learning

âŒ Unethical AI Use (Academic Dishonesty)

  • Copying: Submitting AI-generated text as your own
  • Answers: Having AI solve problems you're supposed to solve
  • Entire assignments: AI does the work, you just submit
  • No attribution: Using AI ideas without documentation
  • Prohibited use: Using AI when teacher explicitly forbids it
  • Exams/tests: Using AI during assessments (unless allowed)
  • Others' work: Having AI complete group member's portion

Key: AI replaces your learning or you misrepresent work as your own

Environmental & Social Impact

ðŸŒ Environmental Costs

  • Energy consumption: Training large AI models uses enormous amounts of electricity
  • Carbon footprint: Equivalent to emissions from multiple cars over their lifetime
  • Water usage: Data centers require massive cooling, using millions of gallons
  • E-waste: Specialized hardware becomes obsolete quickly
  • Resource extraction: Rare earth minerals needed for AI chips

What you can do: Use AI thoughtfully, not wastefully. Ask yourself if AI is necessary for each task.

ðŸ‘¥ Job Displacement Concerns

  • Automation: Some jobs may be reduced or eliminated
  • Skill shifts: Workers need to learn new skills to stay relevant
  • Inequality: Benefits may not be distributed equally
  • New opportunities: AI also creates new types of jobs
  • Human advantage: Creativity, empathy, judgment remain uniquely human

Your role: Develop skills AI can't replaceâ€”critical thinking, creativity, collaboration, emotional intelligence.

âš–ï¸ Digital Divide

  • Access inequality: Not everyone has equal access to AI tools
  • Cost barriers: Advanced AI features often require payment
  • Digital literacy: Not everyone has skills to use AI effectively
  • Language barriers: AI works better in some languages than others
  • Amplifying inequality: Those with access gain advantages

Consider: How can we ensure AI benefits everyone, not just those with access?

AI Error Detection Record

ðŸ‘¨â€ðŸŽ“

Student Critical Thinking Tool

Use when AI gives you suspicious or questionable information

Purpose: This worksheet trains you to spot AI errors, investigate them systematically, and understand WHY AI makes mistakes. It develops healthy skepticism and research skills.

ðŸ“‹ How to Use This Worksheet

  1. Identify suspicious claim: When AI says something that seems wrong, outdated, or too convenient, write it down
  2. Explain your suspicion: What triggered your skepticism? Contradicts prior knowledge? Sounds too simple? Lacks detail?
  3. Research independently: Find TWO credible sources that address the same topic
  4. Document the truth: What do reliable sources actually say? Be specific.
  5. Analyze the error: Why did AI get it wrong? Outdated training data? Misunderstood context? Hallucination?
  6. Learn prevention: How can you avoid this error type in the future? Better prompts? Different questions?

ðŸš¨ Red Flags: AI is likely wrong when it: gives exact statistics without sources, contradicts well-known facts, provides suspiciously perfect answers, or makes claims about recent events.

ðŸ“ Example: Catching an AI Error

AI Statement: "Napoleon Bonaparte was 5'2" tall, making him unusually short for his time."

Why Suspicious: This is a famous "fact" but I've heard it debunked before.

Source 1: Britannica Encyclopedia - Napoleon was 5'6" to 5'7", average height for French men of his era

Source 2: History.com - Confusion arose from French vs. English measurement differences

Actual Facts: Napoleon was average height. The myth came from British propaganda and measurement conversion errors.

Why AI Erred: AI was trained on texts that repeated this common myth without fact-checking it.

How to Avoid: Always verify "famous facts" that seem too interesting. Ask AI for sources, then check those sources independently.

ðŸ‘©â€ðŸ« Teacher Notes

How to Assign: Give students intentionally flawed AI outputs to investigate, OR have them find errors in their own AI interactions. Assign 1-2 error investigations per week.

How to Review: Check that students used credible sources, explained the error mechanism (not just "AI was wrong"), and identified practical prevention strategies.

Discussion Ideas: Create an "AI Error Hall of Fame" where students share the most interesting errors they've found. Discuss patterns: What types of errors are most common? Most dangerous?
